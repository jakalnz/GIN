Gaps-in-Noise Test

Two-Interval Forced-Choice Version (touch friendly)

Instructions

In each trial, you will hear two intervals of white noise. One of the intervals will contain a brief silent gap. Your task is to identify which interval (first or second) contained the gap. Even if you're not sure, please make your best guess — every trial needs a First or Second answer for the results to be valid.

1) Adjust the volume to a comfortable level and confirm the channel is correct.
2) Complete 3 short practice trials (not scored) before the real test begins.
3) The gap duration shrinks after three consecutive correct answers, and grows immediately after any incorrect answer; your threshold is estimated from the staircase's reversal points.

Try to minimize distractions and use headphones for the best experience. This is a two-interval forced-choice task, distinct in design from the single-interval "official" clinical GIN test.

About This Procedure

Task & stimulus

  • Each trial presents two 1-second broadband noise intervals, played back to back. Exactly one interval contains a brief silent gap; the other is continuous noise. Which interval (first or second) contains the gap is randomized each trial.
  • This is a forced-choice task: you must pick First or Second every trial, even if you're not sure. Forcing a guess (rather than offering an "unsure" option) is what makes this design bias-free — it removes the effect of individual differences in willingness to commit to an answer, so the measured threshold reflects genuine sensitivity rather than response caution.
  • Noise is freshly generated per interval per trial, so no waveform repeats.
  • Gap onset/offset use a short (~5ms, or half the gap for very short gaps) linear fade rather than an instant cut, to avoid an audible "click" at the edges that could act as a cue independent of the gap itself.
  • The gap's position within its interval is randomized per trial (kept at least 100ms from the interval's start/end — i.e. somewhere in the middle 800ms of each 1000ms interval), rather than fixed at the interval's midpoint, so its timing can't be anticipated.

Channel routing (ear selection)

  • The Channel selector routes noise to Binaural (both ears), Left Ear Only, or Right Ear Only via a true stereo buffer — the non-selected ear receives silence, not just a quieter signal.
  • Changing the channel mid-session requires re-confirming calibration before the test can continue.

Volume calibration & gating

  • Before any scored trial, you must play continuous calibration noise, adjust the volume slider to a comfortable level, and confirm it sounds correct for the selected channel.
  • Play Trial and the response buttons are disabled (gated) until calibration is confirmed.
  • This standardizes relative comfort and verifies correct ear routing — it does not measure absolute sound pressure level (dB SPL), which depends on your specific headphones/speakers and device volume.

Practice block

  • Immediately after calibration, 3 unscored practice trials run using an easy, large gap so you can get familiar with the two-interval task before anything counts.

Staircase & threshold estimation

  • A 3-down/1-up staircase: the gap only shrinks (harder) after three consecutive correct responses; a single incorrect response (including a wrong guess) immediately grows it (easier) and resets the correct streak. This converges on a higher-accuracy point (~79% correct) than a simpler 1-down/1-up rule (~50% correct), giving a more conservative threshold estimate.
  • The test stops after 8 reversals (points where the staircase's direction flips).
  • Your threshold is estimated as the mean gap duration at the last 6 reversals — the first 2 are discarded, since they reflect the staircase still settling from its starting point rather than steady-state performance.
