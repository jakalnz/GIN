Gaps-in-Noise (GIN) Behavioral Test

v3 — Single-Interval Staircase Method (touch friendly)

Instructions

You will first complete 3 short practice trials (not scored) to get familiar with the task, then the real test begins.

You will be presented with a 1000ms long sound clip when you press "Play Sound" (toggleable to 500ms). Your task is to determine whether there is a gap in the sound. If you hear a gap, click "Yes". If you don't hear a gap, click "No". The test continues until the staircase has reversed direction 8 times; your threshold is estimated from those reversal points (where your responses flip from correct to incorrect or vice versa).

Note: the volume calibration step sets a comfortable, consistent listening level and confirms channel routing — it does not measure absolute sound pressure level (dB SPL), which depends on your headphones/speakers.

About This Procedure

Stimulus

  • Each trial is a freshly generated burst of broadband noise (500ms or 1000ms, selectable), so no waveform repeats across trials.
  • A "gap" is a brief drop to silence within the burst, drawn from a fixed set of candidate durations (0–20ms).
  • Gap onset/offset use a short (~5ms, or half the gap for very short gaps) linear ramp rather than an instant cut, to avoid an audible "click" at the edges that could act as a cue independent of the gap itself.

Randomized gap window

  • The gap's position within the burst is randomized on every trial (kept at least 100ms from the burst's start/end), rather than fixed at the midpoint.
  • This prevents anticipating "when" the gap will occur and forces genuine gap detection rather than timing-based guessing.

Channel routing (ear selection)

  • The Channel selector routes the noise to Binaural (both ears), Left Ear Only, or Right Ear Only via a true stereo buffer — the non-selected ear receives silence, not just a quieter signal.
  • Changing the channel mid-session requires re-confirming calibration before the test can continue, since a channel change can alter perceived loudness/correctness.

Volume calibration & gating

  • Before any scored trial, you must play continuous calibration noise, adjust the volume slider to a comfortable level, and confirm it sounds correct for the selected channel.
  • The Play Sound / Yes / No controls are disabled (gated) until calibration is confirmed.
  • This standardizes relative comfort and verifies correct ear routing — it does not measure absolute sound pressure level (dB SPL), which depends on your specific headphones/speakers and device volume.
  • Clip duration (500ms/1000ms) is a one-time setup choice, not a per-trial control — it locks once calibration is confirmed so trials stay comparable within a session.
  • On desktop, keyboard shortcuts are available once calibration is confirmed: Space plays the sound, Y answers "Yes", N answers "No".

Practice block

  • Immediately after calibration, 3 unscored practice trials run (two easy/large gaps and one no-gap "catch" trial, in random order) so you can get familiar with the task before anything counts.

Staircase & threshold estimation

  • A 1-down/1-up staircase: a correct response shrinks the next gap (harder), an incorrect response grows it (easier).
  • The test stops after 8 reversals (points where the staircase's direction flips) — not after a fixed trial count. This ties test length to the staircase actually having converged, rather than to a fixed number of trials or to a specific response type.
  • Your threshold is estimated as the mean gap duration at the last 6 reversals — the first 2 are discarded, since they reflect the staircase still settling from its starting point rather than steady-state performance.
  • A false positive (saying "Yes" when there was no gap) can only occur once the staircase has been driven down to the floor (0ms gap). It no longer extends the test — it's simply counted and reported in your results, since a test-length penalty tied to one response type doesn't actually improve the threshold estimate and can make sessions unpredictably long.
